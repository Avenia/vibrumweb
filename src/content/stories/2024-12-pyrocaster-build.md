Some instruments announce themselves immediately. The Pyrocaster was quieter about it.

The idea started with a piece of swamp ash — a lighter blank, open grain, exactly the kind of resonant, transparent-sounding body wood that vintage Fender-style builds live or die by. I set it aside for a few weeks before I touched it. I wanted to think about what it should become.

## Body

The blank was worked down by hand. Nitrocellulose is the only finish I'll use on this kind of instrument — it's thinner, it cures slowly, and over time it bonds with the wood in a way that polyurethane simply doesn't. It breathes. You can feel it under your hands.

The routing was done in stages. The control cavity is shielded with copper foil — a small thing, but it matters. Hum in the electronics is a choice, not an inevitability.

## Neck

Hard maple for the neck, Indian rosewood for the fingerboard. Both chosen from a batch I've been working through slowly — I don't buy wood without looking at it.

The profile is a comfortable C. Not aggressive, not baseball-bat thick. It sits in the hand without demanding attention, which is exactly right. The frets were leveled, crowned, and polished by hand. A fret job is the difference between an instrument and a tool.

## Hardware

Nickel throughout. Gotoh SD90 tuners — they lock without looking like they do, which matters aesthetically. The Wilkinson VS100N bridge: a floating tremolo that returns to pitch reliably and doesn't fight you.

Bone nut, cut from scratch. No synthetic substitutes.

## Pickups

Lollar Blackface in the neck, Lollar Blonde in the bridge. Jason Lollar winds these to vintage specs — they're clear, dynamic, and they respond to the player. They clean up when you roll back the volume. They sing when you push them. That's all I ask.

## The Result

The Pyrocaster ended up sounding the way it looks: direct, clear, and a little warmer than you'd expect. It's an instrument that rewards attention and punishes sloppiness — which I consider a feature.
